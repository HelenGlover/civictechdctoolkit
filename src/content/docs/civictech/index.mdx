title: What is Civic Tech
---

There is no universally accepted definition for civic tech because it encompasses a broad range of ideas, services, and products.

So at Civic Tech DC, we define it as:

> People using technology to strengthen civic life.

Unlike traditional “gov tech,” which often focuses on internal government systems, civic tech is typically community-driven and public-interest oriented. It’s about building tools with and for communities to strengthen democracy and everyday civic participation.

In the past, when Civic Tech DC was Code for DC, we regularly used the term “civic hacking.” From the archives of the early 2020s: 

> In its most basic sense, civic hacking means finding creative solutions for civic problems. We often focus on the technical side of things, but that is by no means the only approach, and the best solution often changes from issue to issue. For example, a low-tech civic hack could involve walking around a neighborhood with a clipboard to collect and share data not tracked by the city. Civic hackers often work with the local government and/or community organizations.

For a more in-depth introduction to civic hacking, see check out this chapter in Josh Tauberer’s [Open Government Data: The Book](https://opengovdata.io/2014/civic-hacking/).

## Food for Thought 



#### 1. We designed with the community

Civic tech is about solving problems, but solutions created without explicit community participation can easily miss the realities of the people intended to be supported. Communities are less of “users” in the traditional startup sense, but those who directly experience the problem and hold that trust. At Civic Tech DC, our strongest projects are the ones that continuously validate whether they're actually useful to the people around them. 

This principle cuts both ways. While civic tech is about designing outward for the community, it should also mean designing inward in creating a community for all backgrounds and skills. At the end of 2025, we identified the audiences we most want to see more of at Civic Tech DC. It is not only beneficial to the projects that require skills beyond just technical execution, but also in communicating that the civic space is open for everyone.

---

#### 2. Failure is productive 

Civic tech spaces are very comfortable with experimentation, and with civic tech trying to solve some of the biggest institutional problems, it rarely has clean solutions. At Civic Tech DC, a project that looks like it “failed” in the lack of a tangible product often isn’t. This includes realizing that a proposed solution is poorly scoped and making the responsible call to walk away, to having the project be embedded into an existing process. In a volunteer community, sustainability can be less about constant visible momentum and more about whether the project created reusable knowledge.

---

#### 3. Expanding who we are

Our attendees are mostly aligned in two different camps. On one side are the contributors who want structure and to be part of a team working toward a shared goal. On the other side are the builders who want space to co-work or start up new projects. Traditionally, we have focused on the contributors, but we need to open up space for builders who help the community move forward instead of staying stagnant.

Civic Tech DC has also focused on the nonprofit and community space, and this is not something we will de-emphasize, but complement by expanding our definition of civic tech to the builders and prioritizing work with workforce development groups, startups, and businesses through event programming. The definition of civic tech is fairly loose, and it should reflect the breadth of people trying to make things better. 

---

#### 4. Staying grounded but not stagnant

The root of Civic Tech DC is our projects and should maintain that way, but we should also allow for growth around that core through the events we host and the partnerships we build.

Civic Tech DC sits within a broader ecosystem of change. Civic tech communities like ours prototype ideas and surface problems that institutions can then act on at scale. The institutions we work with, whether government agencies or nonprofits, are where foundational change gets implemented at scale. Our goal is to support the pipeline in helping people learn about civic tech and be part of these institutions.

That means creating educational resources for people who are interested and new to the civic tech space. And it means looking outward toward youth, investing in the next generation of people who will carry this work forward.
